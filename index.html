Binary Systems in the Human Past

                Binary systems are natural to human interpretation of the world around us. The universe is filled, to the brim, with both apparent and true opposites. Through observation, human beings have naturally come to describe the world in terms of opposites: light-dark, hot-cold, big-small, far-near, good-bad, pleasure-pain, day-night, summer-winter, future-past, and the list is seemingly endless. It is thus no surprise that numerous cultures around the world have innovated the use of binary for their own purposes. The most prominent among these would be Geomancy from the medieval Muslim world, and I-Ching from ancient China. Both are binary systems that are used to interpret and to predict outcomes from chance or chaos. Although these cultures did not necessarily view these systems as binary or computational, they can nevertheless be understood as such from our perspective in the 21st century.

I-Ching

                The I-Ching (易經, Yì Jīng) is a 6-bit binary system that originates from ancient China. In it, two opposing states are determined from actions of chance such as flipping coins, in sequence, to create a binary string. As there are a finite number of outcomes, the I-Ching has a specific identity and interpretation for every single outcome of which there are 64 hexagrams (6 bits in succession) which can be further broken down into combinations of 8 possible trigrams (3 bits in succession). Geomancy

                Geomancy is a 4-bit binary system that originates in the medieval pre-Islamic Arab world. 16 lines of random dots are made while a matter in question is described. Then the 16 lines are interpreted as even (0, or two dots) or odd (1, or one dot) based on the number of dots per line. This gives 16 derived bits, which are then distributed to four groups that constitute “mothers”. Then four “daughters” are derived from a transposition of the first, second, third, and fourth bits of the mothers. Then a binary XOR operation is done on the pairs of mothers and pairs of daughters to create four “nieces”. Then a binary XOR operation is done on the pairs of nieces to create two “witnesses”. Lastly the pair of witnesses undergo one last binary XOR operation to create a final “judge” that is used to interpret matters. Click [here] to use my Geomancy simulator and explore it for yourself.

Ifá

                Ifa is a 16-bit binary system that originates in Yoruba culture in western Africa. In this system, either a divining chain with 16 halves of palm nuts are cast and the convex or concave side of a nut is interpreted as odd or even, or a person is to try to swipe a pile of nuts from one hand to another 16 times -- with the remainder per swipe determining the quality of each bit. Once all 16 bits are determined, they can be used to find appropriate prayers and answers in the ritual. Click [here] to use my Ifa simulator and explore it for yourself.

                All these systems are ways in which human beings have taken the chaos of fate and chance and broken it down into a limited number of possibilities that they can thus assign meaning towards, and derive guidance and spiritual comfort from.

Visual Binary

                Although many of these binary-based systems existed in the past, it was not until the work of 17th century polymath, Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz, that binary was formalized as a mathematical system. His work is where binary notation with zeros and ones originates from. Of course, using zeros and ones to represent base-2 is not a crazy leap of logic. It's simply a case of reducing the set of numbers that we use, 0 through 9, down to its first two members.  For bases greater than 10, letters of the alphabet can be used to represent digits greater than 9. Binary Systems in the Human Past
*   Binary systems feel natural to human interpretation of the world.

I-Ching
*   The I-Ching is a 6-bit binary system from ancient China.
*   The system uses chance actions, like coin flips, to determine a sequence of two opposing states, creating a binary string.
*   The I-Ching assigns a unique identity and interpretation to each of its 64 possible outcomes, known as hexagrams.
*   These 64 6-bit hexagrams can be broken down into combinations of 8 possible 3-bit trigrams.

Geomancy
*   Geomancy is a 4-bit binary system from the medieval Muslim world.
*   The process begins by creating 16 lines of random dots while considering a specific question.
*   Each line is interpreted as even (0) or odd (1) based on its dot count.
*   These 16 bits are arranged into four 4-bit groups called the "mothers."
*   Four "daughters" are derived by transposing the bits of the "mothers."
*   Four "nieces" are created by performing a binary XOR operation on pairs of "mothers" and "daughters."
*   Two "witnesses" are created by performing an XOR operation on pairs of "nieces."
*   A final "judge" is created by performing an XOR operation on the two "witnesses" and is used to interpret the matter.

Ifá
*   Ifá is a 16-bit binary system from the Yoruba culture in western Africa.
*   The 16 bits are determined either by casting a divining chain or by repeatedly taking nuts from a pile and noting the remainder.
*   The resulting 16-bit string is used to find appropriate prayers and answers within the ritual.
*   These historical systems are all methods for breaking down the chaos of chance into a finite set of possibilities.
*   By assigning meaning to these possibilities, people derive guidance and spiritual comfort.

Binary Fractals
*   A binary fractal is a bitmap where white represents 1 and black represents 0.
*   This 2D bitmap is generated from a single 1-dimensional binary string.
*   The base pattern of the 1D string is encoded throughout the entire bitmap.
*   For example, the first row of the fractal for 4 (100) is 1-0-0.
*   The first row of the bitmap is identical to the first column.
*   Each subsequent row is either the original base string or its binary complement.
*   The determination of whether a row is the original or its complement depends on the corresponding bit in the first column.
*   The resulting 3x3 bitmap for 4 (100) consists of rows 1-0-0, 0-1-1, and 0-1-1.
*   The "fractal" aspect arises from tiling the base bitmap 2x2 and overlaying a 2x scaled copy using boolean operations.
*   This process of tiling, scaling, and overlaying can be repeated infinitely to create higher "orders" of complexity.
*   The base bitmap with no self-interference is called a "first-order" binary fractal.
*   Further complexity is created when a different binary string interferes with the base fractal.
*   Interference patterns can also be generated by rotating two fractals as they overlay.
*   These rotational interferences create "zones" of repeating patterns whose size depends on the angle.
*   An angle of 180/π degrees produces a zone that is 1 pixel wide.
*   The required angle for a specific zone size is (180/π) divided by the desired size.

Projection onto a "Bipolar" Sphere
*   The first-order fractal patterns have a unique topology.
*   The top-left corner acts as one pole, and the bottom-right corner acts as the opposite pole.
*   The top-left pole radiates the base pattern horizontally and vertically.
*   The bottom-right pole radiates the reverse of the base string's complement.
*   The fractal inherently encodes the base string, its complement, its reverse, and its reverse-complement.
*   A 90-degree counter-clockwise rotation transforms the base string into its complement.
*   A 90-degree clockwise rotation transforms the base string into its reverse.
*   A 180-degree rotation transforms the base string into its reverse-complement.
*   The diagonal lines of the fractal can be mapped as "latitude bands" on a sphere.
*   The number of diagonal latitude bands is equal to (2 · width) - 1.
*   This method allows a 1D string to be projected as information onto a 2D spherical surface.

Binary Taxonomy (The Four Number Types)
*   Integers and their corresponding fractals can be classified into distinct types based on their binary symmetry.
*   The primary categories are Monolateral, Bilateral, and Quadrilateral.
*   A number is Monolateral if its binary form is a palindrome, and all Monolaterals are odd.
*   The Odd Bilateral category includes all odd numbers that are not palindromes.
*   Odd Bilateral numbers always exist in pairs, where one is the exact binary reverse of the other.
*   Even Bilateral numbers are generated by the Reverse Complement Concatenation (RCC) operation.
*   The RCC formula is N = S1 + ¬(~ S1), where S1 is a k-bit string and + is concatenation.
*   Even Bilateral numbers produced by RCC always have an even bit-length (2k).
*   The Quadrilateral category contains all numbers that are not Monolateral or Bilateral.
*   All Quadrilateral numbers are even.
*   A "trilateral" type is impossible in this system due to the inherent symmetries of the square bitmap.

The "Binary Grammar" (The Toolbox)
*   A "Binary Grammar" describes the generation of these number types through a set of operations on a binary string `S`.
*   The three core Actions are: Replace (`→`), Append (`+`), and Prepend (`+`).
*   The three core Operations are: Identity (`I`), Invert (`¬`), and Reverse (`~`).
*   The notation `[OPERATION] S + [OPERATION] S` defines the sequence, where the left side is prepended and the right side is appended.
*   The Reverse-Replace operation `S → ~ S` defines the pairing rule for Odd Bilaterals.
*   The Reverse-Append `S + ~ S` and Reverse-Prepend `~ S + S` operations are generators for Monolaterals.
*   The Identity-Append operation `S + I S` generates "Tandem Palindromes," a sub-class of Even Bilaterals.
*   The Invert-Append operation `S + ¬ S` generates "Contra-Palindromes," another sub-class of Even Bilaterals.
